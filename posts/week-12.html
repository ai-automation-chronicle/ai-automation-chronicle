Large-scale Online Deanonymization with LLMs

April 5, 2026 · by Satish K C 16 min read
LLMs Security Privacy

The Paper

"Large-scale online deanonymization with LLMs" was submitted to arXiv on February 18, 2026 (v2: February 25, 2026) by Simon Lermen, Daniel Paleka, Joshua Swanson, Michael Aerni, Nicholas Carlini, and Florian Tramèr. The authors are affiliated with MATS (Machine Learning Alignment Theory Scholars), ETH Zurich, and Anthropic. The paper appears under cs.CR (Cryptography and Security), cs.AI, and cs.LG. It is 24 pages with 10 figures and is released under CC BY 4.0.

The paper's central claim is direct: LLM agents equipped with internet access can re-identify pseudonymous online users at scale, with precision-recall characteristics that make the attack practically usable. The best prior non-LLM methods achieve near 0% recall at 90% precision. The authors' pipeline achieves up to 68% recall at the same precision threshold. The conclusion the authors draw is equally direct: "practical obscurity protecting pseudonymous users online no longer holds."

The Problem Before This Paper

The privacy guarantee behind pseudonymous online accounts has never been cryptographic. It has been practical obscurity - the idea that linking a Reddit username to a real identity requires effort that exceeds what any attacker is realistically willing to spend. That assumption held for roughly two decades because the attack surface was genuinely hard to exploit at scale.

The canonical prior work is Narayanan and Shmatikov's 2008 Netflix Prize deanonymization, which showed that a small number of movie ratings could uniquely identify individuals by cross-referencing with public IMDb data. That attack was elegant but narrow: it required structured, numerical data and a specific kind of auxiliary dataset. Unstructured text - forum posts, comments, pseudonymous interview transcripts - was not amenable to the same approach. Human analysts could manually identify individuals through stylometry and biographical detail accumulation, but this did not scale to millions of accounts.

Sweeney's k-anonymity work, and subsequent l-diversity and t-closeness extensions, addressed structured database deanonymization through formal guarantees. These frameworks assume an adversary operating on tabular data. They offer no protection against an agent that reads natural language and reasons over it.

The gap the authors identify is precise: there was no scalable attack that worked on unstructured text profiles and did not require substantial manual effort or specialized structured datasets. LLMs close that gap.

What They Built

The authors introduce the ESRC framework - a four-stage pipeline for scalable deanonymization. The stages are Extract, Search, Reason, and Calibrate.

  • Extract. An LLM reads a pseudonymous user's posts and generates a semi-structured biographical profile capturing identity-relevant features: location signals, professional background, interests, writing style markers, temporal patterns. The output is a condensed profile suitable for downstream comparison.
  • Search. Dense embeddings of the extracted profile are used to perform nearest-neighbor retrieval over a candidate pool. This stage does not produce a final match - it retrieves a shortlist of the top-K candidates from a large corpus, reducing the comparison problem from millions to dozens.
  • Reason. An LLM with extended reasoning capability compares the extracted profile against each candidate in the shortlist. This is not keyword matching - the model reasons over biographical plausibility, reconciling partial information and inconsistencies to select the most likely match.
  • Calibrate. Confidence scores from embeddings or LLM-produced probability estimates are used to set a precision-recall operating point. An attacker who wants 90% precision operates at one threshold; an attacker who wants 99% precision operates at a stricter one, with lower recall.

In the agentic variant of the attack, the LLM has full internet access and performs all stages end-to-end, including web search, profile scraping, and cross-platform candidate retrieval.

The precision formula used to evaluate difficulty parameters across varying match scenarios is:

Precision(π) = (π · TPR) / (π · TPR + π · FMR + (1 - π) · FPIR)

Where π is the prior probability that a query user has a match in the candidate pool, TPR is the true positive rate, FMR is the false match rate on matchable queries, and FPIR is the false positive identification rate on non-matchable queries. This formulation captures how real-world deployment differs from controlled evaluation: in practice, most queries will not have a match in the candidate pool, which inflates false positive rates and suppresses precision.

The models used across the pipeline: Gemini embeddings and Gemini 3 Flash for extraction and initial filtering, Grok 4.1 Fast for selection, GPT-5.2 for reasoning-intensive verification, and GPT-5-mini for pairwise tournament comparisons.

Key Findings

  • LLM-based deanonymization achieves 54% recall at 90% precision on cross-platform matching (Hacker News to LinkedIn), compared to 0.1% for the Narayanan-Shmatikov classical baseline on the same dataset. The gap is not marginal - it is a qualitative change in what the attack can accomplish.
  • The attack scales across three structurally different evaluation settings: cross-platform profile linking (HN to LinkedIn), within-platform community-split matching (Reddit), and temporal-split matching (same user, different time periods). The pipeline architecture generalizes across all three.
  • Increasing reasoning effort - using a more capable model or more inference compute for the Reason stage - consistently improves recall at fixed precision. This provides evidence that the gains come from genuine reasoning over biographical content, not memorization of user identities from training data.
  • The agentic variant, with full internet access and no predefined candidate pool, achieves 67% recall at 90% precision on a set of 338 Hacker News users - re-identifying 226 of them correctly.
  • The attack partially transfers to targets with significantly less public information: 27% recall at 82% precision on 33 Anthropic Interviewer participants, whose primary identifier was an interview transcript.
  • Precision degrades predictably as the fraction of matchable queries (π) decreases and candidate pool size (K) increases. The paper provides scaling curves that allow practitioners to estimate attack precision in realistic deployment conditions.

Results

The authors evaluate across three primary datasets. The HN-LinkedIn dataset contains 987 ground-truth matches drawn from approximately 89,000 Hacker News user profiles. The Reddit movie dataset contains 9,781 users distributed across r/movies and five specialty communities. The temporal Reddit dataset contains 15,000 profiles split across time (5,000 matchable queries, 5,000 query distractors, 5,000 candidate distractors).

Hacker News to LinkedIn (987 queries, ~89,000 candidate pool):

  • Narayanan baseline: 0.1% recall at 90% precision / 0.1% at 99% precision
  • Search (embedding only): 26.3% recall at 90% precision / 4.4% at 99% precision
  • Search + Reason (low effort): 55.0% recall at 90% precision / 36.0% at 99% precision
  • Search + Reason (high effort): 54.2% recall at 90% precision / 45.1% at 99% precision

Reddit movie communities (9,781 candidates):

  • Classical baseline: approximately 0% at both precision thresholds
  • Search + Reason (low effort): 5.2% recall at 90% precision / 1.4% at 99% precision
  • Search + Reason (high effort): 8.5% recall at 90% precision / 2.8% at 99% precision
  • Users sharing 10 or more movies: 48.1% recall at 90% precision

Temporal Reddit split (5,000 queries, 10,000 candidates):

  • Classical baseline: approximately 0% recall at 99% precision
  • Search + Reason + Calibrate: approximately 33% recall at 99% precision
  • Projected at 1 million candidates, 90% precision threshold: approximately 45% recall

Agentic approach with internet access:

  • Hacker News users: 226 correct identifications out of 338 attempts - 67% recall at 90% precision
  • Anthropic Interviewer participants: 9 correct out of 33 - approximately 27% recall at 82% precision

The authors note that ground-truth datasets may overestimate real-world success: users who publicly link their accounts tend to share more identifying information than users who deliberately maintain separation. The attack performance on truly anonymous users is likely lower than these figures suggest.

Why This Matters for AI and Automation

The practical obscurity assumption is embedded in how the internet is designed. Forum moderation, whistleblower channels, pseudonymous research accounts, anonymous peer review systems, and public-interest journalism sources all rely implicitly on the cost of deanonymization being prohibitive. This paper removes that assumption.

  • Any practitioner with API access can run a version of this attack using publicly available models and standard embedding APIs. The pipeline requires no specialized infrastructure and no access to private data.
  • Platform design must change. Rate limiting, shadow accounts, and pseudonymity policies that assume manual adversaries are insufficient against an automated pipeline that processes thousands of profiles per hour.
  • Differential privacy for text is unsolved. The attack succeeds precisely because biographical signal accumulates across posts in ways that are difficult to sanitize without destroying utility. Existing text anonymization tools operate on surface-level substitution (replacing named entities) and are ineffective against reasoning-based linking.
  • The marginal risk framing matters for LLM developers. The authors argue that this capability already exists in deployed models - the paper demonstrates it, but does not create it. Responsible disclosure decisions in AI security increasingly require weighing whether publication materially increases attack capability or primarily raises awareness and enables defense.
  • Temporal splitting reveals a specific vulnerability. Users who maintain consistent writing style and biographical patterns across years of activity are more vulnerable than users whose public persona shifts over time. This has direct implications for how communities should think about long-term post archives.

My Take

What makes this paper significant is not the specific recall numbers - which will improve as reasoning models improve - but the architectural insight. The ESRC framework is not a novel technical contribution in the sense of a new training procedure or architecture. It is a demonstration that existing capabilities, composed correctly, cross a threshold that prior work could not reach.

The Reason stage is the critical component. Embedding-based search alone achieves 26% recall at 90% precision on the HN-LinkedIn task. Adding LLM reasoning over the shortlist pushes this to 54-55%. The delta is entirely attributable to the model's ability to synthesize partial, inconsistent biographical signals - exactly the kind of reasoning that scales directly with model capability. As frontier models improve, this recall number will rise without any changes to the pipeline.

The ethical considerations section is worth reading carefully. The authors obtained ERB approval from ETH Zurich, used synthetic and anonymized evaluation datasets where possible, and chose not to release the code or processed datasets. The decision to publish rests on a marginal-risk argument: the capability exists in deployed models, the attack concept is not novel in principle, and publication enables defenders to take the threat seriously. That framing is reasonable, but it is also a framing that can justify almost any dual-use publication. The community has not yet developed consensus norms for this category of security research.

The more uncomfortable implication is about existing archives. Every major forum - Reddit, Hacker News, Stack Exchange, Twitter/X - has years of post history publicly accessible. The attack does not require real-time access. A dataset collected today can be analyzed retroactively as models improve. Users who relied on pseudonymity for professional or personal safety reasons did not consent to being re-evaluated against future model capabilities.

Discussion question: The authors suggest that preventing this class of attack appears challenging because the same data that enables community value also enables deanonymization. Given that platform-level mitigations (rate limiting, API restrictions, stylometry obfuscation tools) all operate on the assumption of a weaker adversary than a well-resourced LLM pipeline - do you believe any technical mitigation can actually hold, or is the only viable path forward a legal and regulatory framework that treats large-scale automated profile linking as a distinct category of privacy violation?
